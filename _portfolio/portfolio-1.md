New York City was scheduled to introduce a congestion fee for vehicles entering the central business district to alleviate traffic congestion starting in June 2024. As New Jersey residents represent a major portion of affected cross-state commuters, understanding their behavioral shifts is essential.

This project explores the impact of congestion pricing on commuter
travel choices from New Jersey to New York City during peak weekday hours using a stated preference survey and segmented mixed logit model. 

### 📌 Research Objective
The implementation of the Central Business District Tolling Program aims to alleviate severe traffic in Manhattan. This project evaluates how interstate commuters from New Jersey react to various tolling schemes, specifically isolating the impact of subjective policy attitudes on transportation mode choice.

### ⚙️ Methodology & Modeling Framework
*(Tip: 插入一张你的方法论架构图，如 MNL/MXL 模型的分类流程)*
![Methodology Flowchart](/assets/images/congestion-methodology.png)

*   **Data Collection:** Conducted a stated preference (SP) survey targeting peak-hour commuters in Hudson County, NJ.
*   **Behavioral Modeling:** Developed Multinomial Logit (MNL) and Mixed Logit (MXL) models to capture unobserved heterogeneity.
*   **Segmentation Strategy:** Commuters were segmented into "Confident" and "Skeptical" groups based on their belief in congestion fee effectiveness.

### 📊 Key Findings
*   **Cost Sensitivity Discrepancy:** Policy skepticism significantly amplifies cost sensitivity. The elasticity of choosing an automobile was **-1.8** for the skeptical group, compared to **-0.8** for the confident group.
*   **Willingness-to-Pay (WTP):** Confident commuters exhibited a higher WTP ($61.35/hour) to reduce travel time compared to skeptical commuters ($47.78/hour).
*   **Required Compensation:** To maintain pre-implementation utility levels, confident segments required a $3.69 compensation, whereas skeptical segments required $2.02.

### 💡 Policy Implications
The quantitative findings suggest that public acceptance is as critical as the pricing scheme itself. Enhancing public trust in the toll's efficacy can mitigate severe behavioral backlash and ensure smoother transition to sustainable transit modes.
